Chapter One

The Beginning

On October 28, 1828 — just eight months after Pottsville officially became a borough — the Miners Bank opened for business. It was the dawn of the anthracite age. Necho Allen had discovered coal on Broad Mountain decades earlier, and by the 1820s, the black diamond rush was transforming the Schuylkill Valley. Welsh and Irish miners, German craftsmen, and Eastern European laborers poured into the region. Pottsville stood at the center of it all.

The bank that opened that October day would outlast empires, survive depressions, and grow alongside a city that earned the title "City of Firsts" — home to America's first anthracite furnace, the first steam locomotive on the Reading Railroad, and the first commercial smelting of iron with anthracite coal. The Miners Bank predates even Yuengling, America's oldest brewery, which opened next door just one year later.

Chapter Two

The Masterpiece

For the bank's hundredth birthday, the directors did something audacious. They hired Cass Gilbert. In 1927, Gilbert was perhaps the most sought-after architect in America. His Woolworth Building had been the tallest in the world. His U.S. Supreme Court building would become the very image of American justice. For Pottsville, Gilbert designed something worthy of the coal country that had powered a nation.

The exterior rose in Harvard brick, anchored by four Doric columns hewn from white Georgia marble. Above the three-story arched windows, keystones bore the carved faces of Mercury — god of commerce — and Pluto — god of mines and the deep earth. At the entrance, ten-foot brass doors depicted miners at work, sculpted by John Polachek, a Hungarian-born artist whose bronzes now reside in the Smithsonian. Those doors alone cost ten thousand dollars — a small fortune in 1927.

"Fourteen types of marble. One unforgettable room."

Inside, the banking hall soared fifty feet to a vaulted ceiling crowned by a glass and bronze skylight. Twelve chandeliers hung from above. The walls were clad in Jeanne d'Arc stone, a French limestone prized for its warm, varied tones. The floors were laid with Roman travertine — quarried from the same Italian hills that supplied the builders of the Colosseum. The countertops were American black walnut, topped with slabs of dark green marble. In all, fourteen distinct varieties of marble were used throughout the building.

Behind the tellers, a vault door weighing thirty-six tons — twenty-seven inches of tool-proof steel, secured by twenty-four bolts and four separate time locks — guarded the wealth of a region.

Chapter Three

The Revival

For nearly a century, the building served as the financial heart of Pottsville. Banks merged, names changed — Miners National became Heritage National, then Sovereign, then Santander. When the last bank closed in 2023, the building's future was uncertain.

Then came a vision: to restore every inch of Cass Gilbert's masterpiece and transform it into something the city had long needed — a luxury hotel and event destination that would keep visitors in Pottsville rather than sending them elsewhere. The renovation has been painstaking, preserving the original materials while weaving in modern comforts. The result is The Miners 1928.
