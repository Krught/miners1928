On a cool October morning in 1828, in a Pennsylvania borough barely eight months old, a bank opened its doors. By the time it celebrated its centennial, it had commissioned one of the greatest architects in American history to build it a masterpiece. This is the story of that building — and of the city, the industry, and the people who made it possible.

Chapter I

The Anthracite Age

1790s–1828

In the final years of the eighteenth century, a hunter named Necho Allen made camp on Broad Mountain in the Schuylkill Valley. He built a fire against an outcropping of black rock and fell asleep. When he woke, the rock itself was burning. What Allen had stumbled upon was anthracite coal — the hardest, cleanest, hottest-burning coal on earth — and his accidental discovery would ignite an industrial revolution that reshaped a nation. Within a generation, the quiet hills of northeastern Pennsylvania would become the energy capital of America, and the small borough that rose at the southern gateway to the coal fields would become one of the wealthiest communities per capita in the United States.

That borough was Pottsville. Founded by John Pott, a tavern keeper and ironmaster who recognized the strategic value of the confluence of the Schuylkill River and the Norwegian Creek, Pottsville was incorporated in February 1828. It was barely a village then — a cluster of frame buildings and mud roads at the foot of Sharp Mountain. But the anthracite beneath its surface would transform it with extraordinary speed. By the 1830s, Pottsville was the undisputed center of an industry that heated every parlor, powered every factory, and fueled every locomotive in the eastern seaboard.

The achievements came quickly, and they were staggering. In 1831, the first steam locomotive of the Philadelphia and Reading Railroad departed from Pottsville, connecting the coal fields to the port city and the wider world. In 1839, Pottsville saw the first successful smelting of iron using anthracite coal — a breakthrough that would help win a civil war and build a continent of railroads. The region produced America's first anthracite furnace, its first breaker machinery, its first innovations in deep-shaft mining. Pottsville became known as "The City of Firsts," and for much of the nineteenth century, it was a place where fortunes were made and history was written.

A temple of business and commerce, raised upon the foundation of coal and iron, standing as testimony to the ambition of a young republic.

— Pottsville Republican, 1868

Waves of immigrants arrived to work the mines and build the towns: Irish fleeing the famine, Germans seeking opportunity, Welsh bringing centuries of mining expertise, and later, waves of Eastern Europeans — Poles, Lithuanians, Ukrainians — whose labor would sustain the industry through its peak decades. They carved communities out of the mountainsides, built churches in every valley, and filled the boarding houses and taverns along Centre Street. The coal region was, for all its hardship, a place of extraordinary human vitality — a meeting ground of cultures, languages, and aspirations.

It was into this world of ambition and energy that the Miners Bank was born. On October 28, 1828 — just eight months after the borough's incorporation, and one year before David G. Yuengling would open his brewery on Fifth Street, establishing what would become America's oldest operating brewery — the Miners Bank opened for business. The bank was, from the very beginning, the financial backbone of the anthracite industry. It financed the sinking of shafts, the construction of breakers, the purchase of mules and timbers and blasting powder. It held the savings of miners and the fortunes of operators. It grew with the community, and the community grew with it.

By the turn of the twentieth century, Pottsville's coal barons had built grand Victorian mansions along Mahantongo Street — an avenue so magnificent it was known as one of the most beautiful residential streets in Pennsylvania. Their wealth, accumulated in the dark tunnels beneath the mountains, flowed through the Miners Bank. And as the bank approached its centennial, its directors asked themselves a question worthy of the age: What kind of building should house an institution of such history?

The answer would arrive from New York, in the person of the most distinguished architect of the era.

Chapter II

The Architect

Cass Gilbert, 1859–1934

Cass Gilbert was born in Zanesville, Ohio, in 1859, and raised in St. Paul, Minnesota. As a young man, he studied architecture at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology under William Robert Ware, one of the founders of American architectural education. But it was his years of travel through Europe — sketching the cathedrals of France, the palazzi of Rome, the public buildings of London — that formed his deepest convictions about the purpose of architecture. He came to believe that great buildings were not merely shelters from the weather. They were instruments of civilization. They expressed the values of the institutions they housed, the aspirations of the communities they served, and the dignity of the democracy that made them possible.

His career was a catalog of American landmarks. In 1895, Gilbert won the competition to design the Minnesota State Capitol in St. Paul — a building that announced, in marble and granite, the ambitions of a young state. The commission launched him into the first rank of American architects and brought him to New York, where he would spend the rest of his career designing buildings that defined the nation's image of itself.

The Cathedral of Commerce

In 1913, Gilbert completed the Woolworth Building in lower Manhattan. Rising 792 feet above Broadway, it was the tallest building in the world — a distinction it held for seventeen years. Sheathed in cream-colored terra cotta and crowned by a copper roof that glowed green against the sky, the Woolworth Building was immediately christened "the Cathedral of Commerce." The name was not ironic. Gilbert had designed the building's lobby as a cruciform hall, with Byzantine mosaics, vaulted ceilings, and marble walls that would not have been out of place in a Venetian basilica. He understood, before almost anyone else, that American commerce had its own grandeur, its own claim to architectural seriousness.

After the Woolworth Building came a succession of major public works: the Alexander Hamilton U.S. Custom House at Bowling Green, with its monumental sculptures by Daniel Chester French; the Detroit Public Library, a masterpiece of Italian Renaissance revival; the campuses of the University of Texas and the University of Minnesota; and federal courthouses in cities across the country. In each, Gilbert demonstrated an almost classical conviction that architecture should elevate the spirit and inspire civic pride.

Architecture is the handwriting of a civilization. We build what we believe, and what we build outlasts us.

— Attributed to Cass Gilbert

The Supreme Court & A Small Town's Grand Ambition

The crowning achievement of Gilbert's career came in 1935, one year after his death, with the completion of the United States Supreme Court Building in Washington, D.C. With its grand portico of Corinthian columns, its bronze doors depicting the history of law, and its inscription — "Equal Justice Under Law" — the building is arguably the most symbolically important structure in American jurisprudence. Gilbert did not live to see it finished, but every line of its design bears his conviction that architecture should serve as a permanent expression of a nation's highest ideals.

It was this architect — the man who built the tallest building in the world, who designed the seat of the nation's highest court — whom the directors of the Miners Bank of Pottsville chose to design their centennial headquarters. The decision speaks volumes about the era, about the prosperity of the anthracite region, and about the pride that a small Pennsylvania coal town felt in its own history and importance. When the Miners Bank commissioned Cass Gilbert, it was declaring, in the most emphatic terms available, that its story mattered — that a bank born in the coal fields of Schuylkill County deserved architecture worthy of a great American institution.

Gilbert accepted the commission. And what he designed was not merely a bank. It was, in every sense, a masterpiece.

Chapter III

The Masterpiece

November 7, 1927

The Miners Trust Company Building opened on November 7, 1927, and from its first day, it was recognized as something extraordinary — not just for Pottsville, not just for the coal region, but for Pennsylvania. Cass Gilbert had created a building that fused Colonial and Greek architectural traditions into something uniquely American, drawing on materials sourced from quarries in Georgia, France, Italy, and the forests of the American interior. Every surface, every detail, every material was chosen with deliberate care, and the result was a building of astonishing richness and refinement.

The Exterior

The building's facade is clad in rich Harvard brick — a dark red-brown brick prized for its unusual depth of color, fired to a density that resists weathering and acquires a subtle patina with age. Against this warm, dark field rise four imposing Doric columns of white Georgia marble, each one a monolith quarried from the hills of northern Georgia and transported by rail to Pottsville. The columns frame the entrance with an authority that is at once classical and distinctly American — Gilbert's signature fusion of European tradition and New World confidence.

Above the three-story arched windows, carved stone keystones bear the faces of Mercury and Pluto — the twin spirits of Pottsville. Mercury, the Roman god of trade and commerce, looks outward toward the markets the bank served. Pluto, the Roman god of mines and the deep earth, gazes downward toward the coal seams that made the region's fortune. Together, they form a symbolic program that announces the building's purpose and honors the source of its wealth: commerce above, coal below, architecture binding both.

The Doors

The building's most celebrated feature greets every visitor before they cross the threshold. The ten-foot twin brass doors were sculpted by John Polachek, a Hungarian-born artist who emigrated to the United States and became one of the country's foremost architectural metalworkers. Polachek's doors depict miners at their labor — figures bent to the coal face, hauling timber, working the seam — a permanent, gleaming tribute to the men whose backbreaking work in the darkness built the fortunes that flowed through this building.

The doors cost $10,000 in 1927 — equivalent to approximately $175,000 today. Polachek went on to found the General Bronze Corporation in New York City, and his work is collected by the Smithsonian Institution's National Portrait Gallery.

The Interior

Step through the brass doors and into the banking hall, and the scale of Gilbert's vision reveals itself. The room rises fifty feet to a vaulted ceiling, crowned by a glass and bronze skylight that floods the space with natural light. Originally, this skylight was the primary source of illumination for the entire hall — a design choice that connected the interior to the rhythm of the day and gave the room the quality of a clerestory in a Gothic cathedral. Twelve grand chandeliers once hung from ornate ceiling medallions, casting supplementary light across the hall. They were removed decades ago during a period of neglect, but they are now being painstakingly restored to their original positions.

The Stone

The walls of the banking hall are clad in Jeanne d'Arc stone, a French limestone quarried near Domrémy-la-Pucelle — the birthplace of Joan of Arc herself. This limestone is prized by architects for its subtle variation in color: warm creams, pale golds, and soft grays that shift in character depending on the quality and angle of the light. It is a stone of extraordinary refinement, and its presence in a bank building in coal-country Pennsylvania is a measure of how seriously Gilbert and his clients took the project.

Beneath your feet lies Roman travertine, quarried from the hills near Tivoli, Italy — the same quarries that supplied the builders of the Colosseum nearly two thousand years ago. Travertine is a sedimentary limestone formed by mineral springs, and its distinctive texture — the small voids and pitting created by gas bubbles trapped during formation — gives it a tactile, almost organic character unlike any other building stone. To walk across these floors is to walk, quite literally, on the same material that paved the Roman Forum.

The original bank countertops were crafted from American black walnut — one of the most prized domestic hardwoods, valued for its deep chocolate color and fine, straight grain — topped with slabs of dark green marble that create a striking visual contrast against the pale stone walls. In total, fourteen distinct types of marble were used throughout the building, creating a palette of color and texture that shifts as you move through the space: creams and golds and greens and blacks, each chosen by Gilbert to create a specific effect in a specific location.

Some buildings are merely constructed. This one was composed — every surface a deliberate note in a symphony of stone, brass, and light.

The Vault

Beneath the banking hall lies the vault — the building's functional heart and one of its most impressive engineering achievements. The vault door weighs thirty-six tons and is forged from tool-proof steel twenty-seven inches thick. It is secured by twenty-four steel bolts, each four inches in diameter, and controlled by four separate time locks with an integrated electric alarm system. According to a 1927 advertisement in the Pottsville Republican, the vault was "absolutely impregnable" — a claim that, nearly a century later, remains difficult to dispute.

Chapter IV

The Revival

2023–2026

When Santander Bank closed its Pottsville branch in March 2023, the building's future was uncertain. For nearly a century, the Miners Trust Company Building had served a single purpose: banking. The teller windows, the vault, the hushed formality of the banking hall — these were the rhythms of its daily life. Now, for the first time in its history, the building stood empty, and the question was simple but urgent: What becomes of a masterpiece when its original purpose is gone?

The answer, it turned out, was not demolition, not subdivision, not abandonment. The answer was restoration — a restoration so thorough, so faithful to Cass Gilbert's original vision, that it amounts to something closer to a resurrection. The vision: to preserve every surface, every material, every detail that Gilbert specified, while transforming the building into what Pottsville has long needed — a luxury hotel and event destination worthy of one of the most extraordinary buildings in Pennsylvania.

The renovation has been painstaking. Original travertine floors have been cleaned and preserved. The Jeanne d'Arc limestone walls have been carefully restored to their original warmth. The brass doors — Polachek's miners, still laboring in their eternal seam — have been polished and refinished. The marble, all fourteen varieties, has been documented, repaired where necessary, and protected for the next century. The grand chandeliers are being reconstructed from historical records and installed in their original ceiling medallions. The skylight, which had been covered over in a previous renovation, is being brought back to life, restoring the banking hall to the cathedral of light that Gilbert intended.

This is not a renovation. It is a resurrection.

Modern systems — HVAC, electrical, plumbing, technology — have been integrated with surgical precision, threaded through the building in ways that serve twenty-first-century comfort without disturbing the architectural fabric. The result is a building that honors its past completely while preparing for a future that its founders could not have imagined but would, one suspects, have heartily approved: a place where people gather to celebrate, to rest, to mark the most important occasions of their lives, surrounded by materials and craftsmanship that connect them to nearly two centuries of American history.

The Miners 1928 opens in Spring 2026. The brass doors are waiting.
